# Sources & References

## Primary Religious Sources

- **The Quran** — Primary Islamic source; Surah Al-Jinn (72) and numerous other references
- **Hadith Collections** — Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, and other canonical collections contain narrations about jinn

## Classical Islamic Scholarship

- Ibn Taymiyyah (1263-1328). Various treatises on jinn and spirits.
- Al-Shibli. *Akam al-Marjan fi Ahkam al-Jann* (Medieval period)
- Al-Jahiz. *Kitab al-Hayawan* — Includes discussion of jinn

## Modern Academic Works

- Lebling, R. (2010). *Legends of the Fire Spirits: Jinn and Genies from Arabia to Zanzibar*. I.B. Tauris. ISBN: 978-1845119935
- El-Zein, A. (2009). *Islam, Arabs, and the Intelligent World of the Jinn*. Syracuse University Press. ISBN: 978-0815650706
- Crapanzano, V. (1973). *The Ḥamadsha: A Study in Moroccan Ethnopsychiatry*. University of California Press.

## Folklore & Cultural Studies

- Marzolph, U. & van Leeuwen, R. (2004). *The Arabian Nights Encyclopedia*. ABC-CLIO.
- Various entries in *Encyclopedia of Islam*

## Documentary & Media

- Various Arabic-language documentaries on jinn traditions
- Academic lectures on Islamic demonology

---

## Note on Sources

Jinn are a matter of religious belief in Islam. Academic sources approach the topic from anthropological, theological, and comparative religion perspectives. Primary sources (Quran, Hadith) are considered divine revelation by Muslims.
